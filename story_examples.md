### Animals & Nature

## The Clever Fox and the Thirsty Crow

Deep in a sunny forest, a crow found a tall clay jug with a little water at the very bottom. She tipped her beak in, but the water was too low to reach. She tipped the jug, but it was too heavy to move.

A fox trotted by and watched with one eyebrow raised. "Having trouble, feathers?"

"The water's too low," sighed the crow, "and I can't tip the jug without spilling it all into the dirt."

The fox thought about helping — foxes are clever, after all — but foxes also like naps more than they like helping crows. "Good luck with that," he said, and curled up under a tree instead.

The crow looked around and spotted a pile of little pebbles by the roots of an old oak. One by one, she picked them up in her beak and dropped them into the jug — plink, plink, plink. With every pebble, the water rose a little higher.

By the time the fox opened one sleepy eye again, the crow was happily sipping cool water from the very top of the jug.

"How did you—" the fox started.

"Didn't need cleverness," said the crow, wiping her beak. "Just patience, and a lot of small pebbles."

## తెలివైన నక్క మరియు దాహంతో ఉన్న కాకి [te]

ఎండ తగిలే అడవిలో, ఒక కాకికి కింద కొద్దిగా నీళ్ళు ఉన్న పొడవైన మట్టి కుండ కనిపించింది. అది ముక్కు లోపలికి పెట్టింది, కానీ నీళ్ళు అందలేదు. కుండను వంచడానికి ప్రయత్నించింది, కానీ అది చాలా బరువుగా ఉంది.

ఒక నక్క అటుగా వచ్చి, కనుబొమ్మ పైకి లేపి చూసింది. "ఏమైంది, పక్షమా?"

"నీళ్ళు చాలా కిందికి ఉన్నాయి," కాకి నిట్టూర్చింది, "కుండ వంచితే నీళ్ళు మట్టిలో పారిపోతాయి."

నక్క సహాయం చేయాలా అని ఆలోచించింది — నక్కలు తెలివైనవి కదా — కానీ నక్కలకు నిద్ర పోవడం ఇష్టం, సహాయం చేయడం కంటే. "మంచిది, నీ ప్రయత్నం చెయ్యి," అని చెప్పి ఒక చెట్టు కింద పడుకుంది.

కాకి చుట్టూ చూసి, ఒక పాత చెట్టు వేరుల దగ్గర చిన్న గులకరాళ్ళు కనిపెట్టింది. ఒక్కొక్కటి ముక్కుతో తీసుకొచ్చి కుండలో వేసింది — టప్, టప్, టప్. ప్రతి రాయితో నీళ్ళు కొద్దిగా పైకి వచ్చాయి.

నక్క నిద్ర లేచేసరికి, కాకి కుండ పైభాగం నుండి హాయిగా నీళ్ళు తాగుతోంది.

"అది ఎలా—" నక్క అడగబోయింది.

"తెలివి అక్కర్లేదు," కాకి తన ముక్కు తుడుచుకుంటూ చెప్పింది. "ఓపిక చాలు, కొన్ని చిన్న రాళ్ళతో."

## चतुर कोल्हा आणि तहानलेला कावळा [mr]

एका उन्हाळ्या जंगलात, कावळ्याला एक उंच मातीचं भांडं सापडलं, ज्यात तळाशी थोडंसं पाणी होतं. त्यानं चोच आत घातली, पण पाणी खूप खाली होतं. भांडं वाकवायचा प्रयत्न केला, पण ते खूप जड होतं.

एक कोल्हा तिथून जात होता, आणि भुवया उंचावून बघत राहिला. "काय झालं, पंखवाल्या?"

"पाणी खूप खाली आहे," कावळा उसासा टाकत म्हणाला, "आणि भांडं वाकवलं तर सगळं पाणी मातीत सांडेल."

कोल्ह्यानं मदत करावी का असा विचार केला — कोल्हे चतुर असतातच — पण कोल्ह्यांना झोप जास्त आवडते, मदतीपेक्षा. "शुभेच्छा," असं म्हणून तो झाडाखाली झोपला.

कावळ्यानं आजूबाजूला बघितलं, आणि एका जुन्या झाडाच्या मुळाशी छोटे छोटे खडे दिसले. एक एक करून चोचीनं उचलून भांड्यात टाकले — टप, टप, टप. प्रत्येक खड्यासोबत पाणी थोडं थोडं वर आलं.

कोल्हा जागा झाला तेव्हा, कावळा भांड्याच्या अगदी वरून आनंदानं थंड पाणी पीत होता.

"हे कसं—" कोल्हा विचारू लागला.

"चतुराई नाही लागली," कावळा चोच पुसत म्हणाला. "फक्त धीर, आणि भरपूर छोटे खडे."

## The Brave Little Mouse

In a cozy burrow at the edge of a wheat field lived the smallest mouse in the whole mouse family. Everyone called her Tiny, and Tiny hated being called Tiny, because it always came with a "you're too small for that."

One stormy night, the family's food store — a whole winter's worth of seeds — got swept toward the rushing creek at the edge of the field. The big mice were too heavy to cross the wobbly old branch that bridged the water. The middle mice were too scared. But Tiny was small enough, and light enough, and — though nobody expected it — braver than anyone had guessed.

She crept out onto the branch, one tiny paw at a time, rain pattering on her ears, the creek roaring below. Halfway across, a gust of wind rocked the branch, and her heart pounded so hard she thought the whole forest could hear it. But she thought of her family's empty food store, took a breath, and kept going.

She reached the far side, nudged the drifting seed sack free from a snag of roots, and — paw over paw — dragged it back across the branch to safety.

That winter, nobody called her Tiny anymore. They called her the bravest mouse in the burrow, and she never once let it go to her head — mostly.

### Silly & Funny

## The Dragon Who Was Afraid of Sneezing

Everyone knows dragons breathe fire when they're angry. But nobody warned the villagers about Sniffles the dragon, who breathed fire whenever he sneezed — and Sniffles had the worst allergies of any dragon in the kingdom.

The trouble was, Sniffles loved flowers. He loved smelling them, rolling in them, wearing little wreaths of them on his enormous scaly head. And every single time, without fail: "AH— AH— AHCHOOOO!" and a fireball would go rocketing across the meadow, scorching haystacks, startling sheep, and once, memorably, cooking the mayor's entire hedge into the shape of a very confused-looking rabbit.

The village decided something had to be done. The baker suggested giving Sniffles a giant handkerchief. The blacksmith suggested a giant clothespin for his nose. Neither worked — the handkerchief caught fire, and the clothespin just made his sneezes come out sideways.

It was the youngest child in the village, a girl who fed the ducks every morning, who finally figured it out. "What if he just doesn't sniff the flowers so close?" she asked. "My nose gets itchy too if I stick it right in."

So they built Sniffles a special long-handled flower-smelling stick — flowers on one end, safely far from his snout on the other. Sniffles could finally enjoy every bloom in the kingdom without setting a single haystack on fire, and the mayor's hedge, once it grew back, was left very much alone.

## The Great Sock Mystery

In the Thompson house, socks kept disappearing. Not both socks — just one from every single pair, leaving behind a drawer of lonely, mismatched partners. Stripy socks with no match. Polka-dot socks with no match. Even Dad's serious grown-up business socks, gone, one at a time.

Milo, age seven and a very serious detective (in training), decided to crack the case. He set up a stakeout behind the laundry basket with a flashlight and a notebook that said CASE FILES in wobbly marker letters.

At midnight, he heard it: a tiny rustle, a tiny squeak, and out from a crack behind the dryer crept the culprit — a small family of mice, dragging a single fuzzy sock toward their den, using it as the world's coziest blanket.

Milo didn't shout. He didn't even move much. He just watched as the tiniest mouse tucked itself into the sock like a sleeping bag, and he found, much to his own surprise, that he didn't want to catch them at all.

The next morning, Milo told his family the mystery was solved, but he never told them exactly how. Instead, once a month, he'd quietly leave one old, unmatched sock by the crack in the wall — and the Great Sock Mystery was never quite a mystery, or a problem, again.

### Adventure & Exploring

## The Map with No Ending

Zara found the map tucked inside an old book at her grandmother's house — yellowed paper, curling at the edges, with a dotted line that wound through forests and over mountains and off the edge of the page entirely, with no "X marks the spot" anywhere to be found.

"Where does it go?" Zara asked.

Grandma smiled the way grandmothers do when they know something wonderful. "That's the best part. Nobody's ever finished it. It just keeps going wherever the adventure takes you."

So Zara decided to follow it — first through the "forest," which turned out to be the tall ferns at the back of the garden, where she discovered a beetle with a shell that shimmered like a tiny rainbow. Then over the "mountain," which was really the big grassy hill by the fence, where the wind was so strong it nearly stole her hat, and from the top she could see the whole neighborhood spread out like its own map.

Every day after that, Zara added a little more to the dotted line herself — the creek behind the school, the crooked tree that looked like a dragon, the spot where the best blackberries grew in July. The map never did have an ending.

Years later, Zara gave it to her own kid, with one more line drawn on: "The best adventures are the ones you keep going on."

## The Kite That Wanted to Touch a Cloud

Every kite in the shop dreamed of flying high, but Ren, a small red kite with a slightly crooked tail, dreamed bigger than the rest: Ren wanted to touch a real cloud.

The other kites laughed, gently. "Clouds are impossibly high," said a fancy purple kite. "Nobody's tail is that strong."

But on a bright, blustery Saturday, a boy named Theo picked Ren from the shelf — not because he was fancy, but because his crooked tail reminded Theo of his own slightly crooked front tooth, and Theo liked things that were a little different.

Out in the field, Theo ran and ran, and Ren caught the wind and rose — past the treetops, past the rooftops, higher than any kite Theo had ever flown. Ren strained against the string, climbing and climbing toward a soft white cloud drifting low that day.

He never quite touched it — the string was only so long, and Theo held tight, because a kite that flies away is a kite that's lost forever. But close enough that a wisp of cloud brushed his crooked tail, cool and damp and soft as a whisper.

Ren didn't mind that he hadn't quite touched it. He'd gotten closer to a cloud than any kite in the shop ever had, and that, he decided, was close enough to call it true.

### Gentle Bedtime

## The Lighthouse Who Kept a Promise

At the edge of a quiet bay stood an old lighthouse named Pip, whose one job, every single night, was to keep his light shining so the fishing boats could find their way home safely.

Pip loved his job, but some nights, when the fog rolled in thick and grey and the wind howled and Pip felt very small against the enormous dark sea, he grew a little afraid. What if his light wasn't bright enough? What if a boat got lost anyway?

One such foggy night, a small fishing boat named the Marigold was struggling to find the harbor, tossed about by waves, her captain squinting hard into the grey. Pip felt the fear rise in his old stone walls — but he thought of every sailor who'd ever trusted his light, and instead of dimming with worry, he shone brighter than he ever had before, steady and warm and sure.

Through the fog, the Marigold's captain caught the glow — faint at first, then clearer — and steered gently toward home.

That night, safe in harbor, the captain looked back up at Pip glowing softly against the dark and murmured, "Thank you, old friend," before heading home to bed.

And Pip, glowing warm and quiet over the sleepy bay, felt the same thing every night after: not fear, but the calm, gentle kind of pride that comes from keeping a promise, even when it's hard.

## The Blanket Fort at the End of the Day

When the sun dipped low and painted the sky in soft oranges and pinks, that was when Amara and her little brother Kofi built their fort — same blankets, same three chairs, same flashlight every single evening, because the best forts, they'd decided, were the ones you already knew by heart.

Inside, everything felt softer. The world outside got quieter. Kofi always brought his stuffed elephant, who — according to Kofi — had traveled to the moon, the bottom of the ocean, and once, memorably, "a birthday party for a very fancy dragon."

Tonight, in the warm flashlight glow, Amara whispered the fort's own bedtime ritual: "What was the best part of today?"

Kofi thought hard, the way seven-year-olds think hard about very important questions. "The part right now," he said. "In the fort. With you."

Amara smiled and pulled the blanket a little higher over both of them. Outside, the stars were coming out one by one, patient and unhurried, the same as they did every night — and inside the fort, the day settled softly down to sleep, the same as it did every night too, safe and warm and exactly as it should be.

### Sharing & Kindness

## The Last Slice of Mango

At recess, Priya unwrapped her favorite snack: sweet, golden mango, cut into perfect little slices. She counted them — five, just enough for her.

Her friend Dev sat down next to her, lunchbox empty except for a plain sandwich. "My mom forgot to pack fruit today," he said, not complaining, just a little sad about it.

Priya looked at her five slices. Five felt like exactly enough a moment ago. Now it felt like maybe it could be four, and four could still be plenty.

"Here," she said, holding one out. "Mangoes are better shared anyway."

Dev's face lit up. "Really? Thanks, Priya!"

They sat together crunching happily, and Priya noticed something funny: the mango didn't taste like less. It tasted like more, somehow — like the best slice of the whole bunch, because she got to watch Dev enjoy it too.

From that day on, snack time at their table became a sharing table, and nobody ever seemed to end up with less than they started with — they just ended up with more friends to share it with.

## మామిడి పండు చివరి ముక్క [te]

విరామ సమయంలో, ప్రియ తనకు ఇష్టమైన తీపి, పసుపు రంగు మామిడి ముక్కలను విప్పింది. ఒకటి, రెండు, మూడు, నాలుగు, ఐదు — తనకు సరిపడా లెక్కపెట్టుకుంది.

తన స్నేహితుడు దేవ్ పక్కన కూర్చున్నాడు, అతని లంచ్‌బాక్స్‌లో సాదా శాండ్‌విచ్ తప్ప పండు ఏమీ లేదు. "అమ్మ ఈరోజు పండు పెట్టడం మర్చిపోయింది," అన్నాడు, ఫిర్యాదు చేయకుండా, కొంచెం బాధగా.

ప్రియ తన ఐదు ముక్కల వైపు చూసింది. ఒక్క నిమిషం క్రితం ఐదు సరిపోతాయని అనిపించింది. ఇప్పుడు నాలుగు అయినా బాగానే ఉంటుందని అనిపించింది.

"ఇదిగో," అని ఒక ముక్క అందించింది. "మామిడి పండు పంచుకుంటేనే బాగుంటుంది కదా."

దేవ్ ముఖం సంతోషంతో వెలిగిపోయింది. "నిజంగానా? థాంక్స్, ప్రియా!"

ఇద్దరూ కలిసి సంతోషంగా తిన్నారు, ప్రియకు ఒక విచిత్రమైన విషయం అనిపించింది: మామిడి పండు తక్కువగా అనిపించలేదు. ఎందుకో ఎక్కువగా అనిపించింది — దేవ్ ఆనందంగా తినడం చూడటం వల్ల, అదే మొత్తంలో అత్యుత్తమ ముక్కలా అనిపించింది.

ఆ రోజు నుండి, వాళ్ళ బల్ల దగ్గర స్నాక్ టైమ్ ఒక పంచుకునే బల్ల అయ్యింది, మరియు ఎవరూ తక్కువతో మిగలలేదు — వాళ్ళు ఎక్కువ స్నేహితులతో మిగిలారు.

## शेवटचा आंब्याचा तुकडा [mr]

सुट्टीच्या वेळी, प्रियाने आपला आवडता गोड, पिवळा आंबा उघडला, सुंदर तुकड्यांमध्ये कापलेला. एक, दोन, तीन, चार, पाच — तिने मोजले, स्वतःसाठी अगदी पुरेसे.

तिचा मित्र देव शेजारी बसला, त्याच्या डब्यात साधं सँडविच सोडून काहीच नव्हतं. "आईने आज फळ द्यायला विसरली," तो म्हणाला, तक्रार न करता, थोडा नाराज होऊन.

प्रियाने आपल्या पाच तुकड्यांकडे बघितलं. क्षणभरापूर्वी पाच पुरेसे वाटत होते. आता चार सुद्धा पुरेसे वाटले.

"हे घे," ती एक तुकडा देत म्हणाली. "आंबा वाटून खाल्ला की जास्त मजा येते."

देवचा चेहरा आनंदाने उजळला. "खरंच? धन्यवाद, प्रिया!"

दोघे मिळून आनंदाने खाल्ले, आणि प्रियाला एक गंमतीशीर गोष्ट जाणवली: आंबा कमी वाटला नाही. उलट जास्त वाटला — देवला आनंदाने खाताना बघून, तो तुकडा जणू सगळ्यात चविष्ट वाटला.

त्या दिवसापासून, त्यांच्या टेबलवरचा खाऊचा वेळ वाटण्याचा वेळ बनला, आणि कुणीही कमी घेऊन संपवलं नाही — फक्त जास्त मित्र घेऊन संपवलं.

## The Puddle Umbrella

It rained hard on the walk home from school, and Wren had forgotten her umbrella. She hunched her shoulders and hurried along, getting wetter with every step.

Ahead of her, a boy from her class named Tobias was walking slowly under a huge striped umbrella, plenty of room to spare.

Wren almost didn't say anything — she didn't know him very well, and asking felt a little scary. But the rain was cold, and she took a breath and caught up to him. "Could I maybe share your umbrella? Just till the corner?"

Tobias looked surprised, then smiled and tilted the umbrella her way. "Sure! I like company anyway. Walking alone in the rain is kind of boring."

They talked the whole way — about their favorite cartoons, and whether puddles were better to jump in with boots or without (boots, they agreed, obviously) — and by the time they reached Wren's street, she wasn't thinking about the rain at all.

"Thanks for sharing," Wren said.

"Thanks for asking," said Tobias. "I almost never get to talk to anyone on the walk home."

They waved goodbye, both a little less alone than they'd been five minutes before — and both looking forward to sharing the walk again the next rainy day.

### Feelings & Patience

## The Jar of Fireflies

Niah wanted to catch a firefly so badly she could barely stand still. Every summer evening, the little lights blinked across the backyard, and every summer evening, her jar came up empty.

"They're too fast," she huffed, flopping down on the grass. "I never catch anything."

Her grandfather sat down beside her, unhurried. "Fireflies don't like to be chased," he said. "They like you to wait. Sit real still, jar open, and let them come find you."

Niah didn't believe him, but she was tired of chasing, so she tried it — sat cross-legged in the cool grass, jar open in her lap, and waited. It felt like forever. Her legs got itchy. She almost gave up twice.

Then, softly, a small green light drifted down and landed right on the rim of her jar, blinking slowly, unbothered, curious.

Niah held very still and let it wander in on its own.

"See?" said her grandfather, smiling. "Some things can't be caught. They have to be waited for."

Niah watched her one small firefly glow inside the jar for a while, then gently let it back out into the dark to blink its way home — deciding that the waiting had turned out to be the best part of catching it after all.

### Helping Friends

## The Wheel That Wouldn't Turn

Oscar's bike wheel got stuck halfway down the hill — a twig jammed right in the spokes — and no matter how hard he pedaled, it wouldn't budge. He was going to be late for the park, and his eyes started to sting with frustrated tears.

His neighbor Fatima, riding just behind him, stopped without being asked. "Hop off, let's look."

Together they crouched by the wheel. Fatima held the bike steady while Oscar wiggled the twig loose, bit by bit, both of them getting a little muddy in the process.

"Got it!" Oscar said, holding up the twig like a trophy.

"Told you two heads are better than one," said Fatima, grinning, wiping a smudge of dirt off her cheek.

They rode the rest of the way to the park together, side by side instead of Oscar rushing ahead like usual, and Oscar found he didn't mind arriving a little late at all — not when the ride there had a friend in it.

### Listening & Following Directions

## The Treasure Hunt That Almost Wasn't

Grandma set up a backyard treasure hunt with clues taped to trees, and Jonah was so excited he sprinted off before she'd even finished explaining the rules.

The first clue said: "Look low, not high, where the garden hose lies." Jonah, still not really listening, searched up in the branches instead — nothing.

He came back grumpy. "This game is broken!"

Grandma knelt down to his height. "Want to try something? Let's read the whole clue together, slowly, before you run anywhere."

Jonah took a breath and listened — really listened — this time. "Look low, not high..." He glanced down at the coiled garden hose by the fence. Tucked right beside it: the next clue.

"I found it!" he shouted.

"You sure did," said Grandma. "Turns out the clues were never broken — you just have to hear the whole thing before you go dashing off."

Jonah slowed down for the rest of the hunt, listening to every clue all the way through before moving, and found every single one — plus a little bag of chocolate coins waiting at the end, which tasted extra sweet for a treasure hunt he'd almost given up on.
